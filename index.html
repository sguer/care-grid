Relational Futures: Why AI Stands for Allostatic Intelligence

Andy Sabrina Guerrier (she/her/elle) · June 2026

Relational foresight is the practice of sensing thresholds, obligations, and future viability through shared, community‑held, ecological, and intergenerational relations. It differs from predictive foresight in four ways:

  • it is threshold‑based, not probabilistic;
  • it is co‑governed, not centralized;
  • it is embedded in Land, not abstracted;
  • it is accountable to intergenerational solvency, not short‑term optimization.

Allostatic Intelligence (not Artificial Intelligence)

Artificial Intelligence forecasts, optimises, and scales. Allostatic Intelligence senses, redistributes, and co-designs.

Allostasis is how a living system maintains continuity through change — not by returning to a fixed point (homeostasis), but by anticipating need and mobilising resources in advance. Allostatic Intelligence names the distributed, relational capacity of a community to do the same:

  • Sense where load is accumulating, before it becomes crisis.
  • Redistribute surplus capacity along ties of trust and reciprocity.
  • Buffer nodes nearing depletion without requiring them to ask each time.
  • Amplify regenerative signals, so healing spreads like the cluster of depletion — but in reverse.

I came to this not through academic reasoning alone. Through my own health journey, I learned to track my body as a sensing system under uncertainty — sparse signals, shifting baselines, non-linear effects, the system being my own body. The question was always: what is being maintained, at what cost, and what fails when compensation runs out? That is the same question the Care Grid asks at community scale.

Reimagining Resilience

In 2021, my mother, a nurse clinician, opened a seniors' residence during COVID-19 lockdowns. It was her dream, and I led the finance and operations. Because she prioritized high-quality care above all else, our margins were non-existent. The goal was never profit, but it meant we had no wiggle room to even begin putting aside money to acquire the property while we were also holding other jobs. When the owner sold the building in 2022, we didn't have the capital to relocate. My mom spent the following year ensuring every resident found a new home and I worked to ensure every liability was cleared.

What I find so relevant to foresight and futures thinking is that I had my eye on everything: finances, information systems, and operational metrics. Yet, I completely bypassed my own embodied experience. The cost I didn't track was my own health, which declined sharply inside that work. No system, including my own, detected the toll until after the doors were closed.

The grief was heavy because this was also a personal dream of community. My paternal grandmother was active there and found ways to endear herself to everyone despite the language barrier. I had started dreaming about a space where she could belong as her capacities changed. I pictured an extension of home where my parents could eventually age with dignity and cultural safety.

I've only recently started speaking about this through a lens of transformation rather than failure. It marked the moment I realized that when proximity care isn't set up to tend to the people providing it, the extra energy required to build resilience has to come from somewhere. Usually, it comes directly out of the people holding the community together.

The Care Grid: A Storytelling Canvas

The Care Grid is the visual and narrative instrument that makes relational viability legible, through reciprocity and thresholds of capacity. It serves allostasis: showing when adaptive capacity is accumulating, thinning, or being redistributed, so that communities can protect equitable and sustainable living conditions under deep uncertainty.

The framework is grounded in a relational view of resilience. Stability is understood as the ongoing capacity to adapt through relationship, reciprocity, and shared sensing. In this sense, the Care Grid is concerned with the conditions that make adaptation possible: trust, material access, cultural continuity, and the ability to respond without losing future options.

Care Grid — interactive prototype

36 nodes across four relational clusters (A–D). Edges encode trust, reciprocity, shared sensing, kinship. Position emerges from the ties; geography plays no role. States are encoded by both colour and shape (● regenerating, ■ buffering, ▲ stressed, ◆ depleted). Click any node to perturb it.

What you see: nodes change colour and shape as their internal capacity moves through four states.

What you can do: click any node to perturb it; "Apply shock" hits an entire relational cluster (a funding cut, a policy change, a local disaster — the kind of shock would be community-specified); "Amplify load" simulates a persistent increase in baseline draw. The story panel narrates what the pattern means in plain language. The decision what to do lives with the community, outside the field-directed graph.

What it does not do: it does not forecast and it does not optimise. It makes thresholds visible so the community can decide.

A footnote on what this is not. The Care Grid is not a climate model. The shocks applied here are relational stressors — they may have climate, economic, or political origins, but the framework is agnostic to the physical mechanism. That is an intentional feature: the same relational architecture can stress-test responses to funding cuts, policy reorganisations, and ecological shifts alike.

Math as a relational practice — why I write from inside the Care Grid

I live this work. I am self‑employed and navigate functional constraints around energy and mobility — I do not drive — and the way my life works is through reciprocal exchange across asymmetric capacities. I offer financial literacy, data analytics, and small automated tools for friends who, in turn, help me get around the city, share research opportunities, and hold parts of my life I cannot hold alone. Multidirectional care and capacity pooling are therefore the fabric of my daily life. I write from inside the experience the framework is trying to make legible.

When I use the phrase math as a relational practice, I mean something simple: the Care Grid is articulated from the same relational conditions it describes. Uneven capacities, shared sensing, and the ongoing work of keeping one another afloat are not abstractions for me. They are the conditions through which I live, care, and dream. Recipes of Memories, on the next tab, is the same framework written in the register of cultural renewal and survival. Quantitative tools, as I use them, are built to hold relational accountability. Everything I build could be drawn by hand or run on the kind of assemble‑yourself PC my father had as his first personal computer — the one on which I first learned how to use a computer at all.

None of this requires massive computing power, or deep learning. The latter can be useful for some kinds of pattern recognition but not for the kind of relational sensing this work asks for. My professional experience in statistical analysis engrained the reflex "can you put it on a map?" While that skill has its place, I had to shed the default of geographic determinism — especially the assumption that colonial boundaries drawn on surveys supersede the authority of communities who define adjacency by trust, kinship, and shared sensing. What I continue to hone is not the production of complex equations or impressive interactive maps, but the ability to sense how people carry responsibilities across unevenly shared burdens, and to continue to practice mathematics as story holding.

Recipes of Memories: Before, During, After the Smoke

Andy Sabrina Guerrier (she/her/elle) — Fellow, Brain Climate & Equity Collaborative

A Note on Land, Water and Foodways

I write from Turtle Island, or so-called Canada, on the traditional and unsurrendered territory of the Anishinaabe Algonquin Nation. Within earshot of the Kichi Sibi — the Ottawa River — pathways wind through the oaks, carrying their own currents: cyclists, walkers, runners, all converging toward the water.

The river's voice meets these shifting human rhythms, carrying the long memory of the Land and drawing my ancestral roots into the cadence of my days. The Latibonit flows through the stories my father shared: the fierce and silted river whose current ran wide through the Artibonite Valley in Haiti. The Taíno, original stewards of the Caribbean islands, knew it as Ja'tibonicu, the Great High Place of the Sacred Waters.

In the sediment of these revered waters, the mazonbèl took to the soil of Hispaniola through forced dispossession and displacement. Preserved as a memory crop in the grounds where African ancestors carved out survival, this tuber naturalized into Indigenous Caribbean and Haitian foodways as a practice of subsistence.

Yet this lineage of resilience now meets a changing climate. Caught between the memory of abundance and the stresses of a warming world, the mazonbèl faces a dual threat: the salt of rising tides and the spread of taro leaf blight, a destructive pathogen that collapses whole harvests.

What follows are three recipes held in memory across the wildfire seasons: the calm before the smoke, the heavy air during those days of poor air quality, and the slow recovery that comes after. I have felt the weight of those seasons in the sealed air of my home, where familiar foodways offer a small sanctuary. Peeling the rough skin of a mazonbèl alongside my grandmother, Granma, reminds me that routine can be a steadying force in uncertain times.

A resilient kitchen carries warm knowledge that nourishes us not just in body, but in the breath we reclaim with every shared recipe.

I. Mazonbèl Bouyi ak Sòs Pwa Nwa — Before

For the days when Granma makes her slow loop around the neighbourhood, sometimes wandering a bit farther. The air quality index sits comfortably low, letting each unhurried breath carry her forward.

Setup

Portable Induction Cooktop
A surface with LED display (300–1800W). With an extension cord, it travels to the sunroom or the backyard when conditions permit.
A heavy-duty extension cord
Provides the reach to prioritize natural ventilation.
Two Heavy-Bottomed Ferrous Pots
Essential for induction. With tight-fitting lids, they hold heat long after the power is cut.

Ingredients

  • 2–3 mazonbèl tubers, peeled under running water
  • Water, enough to cover by two fingers
  • 1 cup dry black beans
  • 2 tablespoons of épis (If the jar is running low, stick to one tablespoon)
  • A sprig of thyme and a whole scotch bonnet poked with two cloves
  • Small handful of fresh parsley, stems and leaves
  • 2 tablespoons white vinegar
  • 3 tablespoons olive oil
  • Salt, a cautious pinch (Granma has high blood pressure)

Method

  1. Peel the roots in large, confident strokes.

    Any worry about not doing it the way Manman does can wait. The skin comes off easier when the tuber is firm and fresh.

  2. Cook the beans.

    In a heavy pot, boil the dry beans in plenty of water. Whenever they reach a hard boil, add a cup of cold water to shock the skins and help them soften. Repeat this until the beans are tender and the liquid is dark and inky. Mash a handful of the softened beans through a strainer into a paste to thicken the base.

  3. Boil the mazonbèl.

    While the beans cook, place the tubers in the second pot with the thyme and salted water. Set the portable induction cooktop to High just until the first bubbles break the surface. Once it boils, cover the pot with a tight lid and drop the setting to a Low Simmer. Set the LED display for 30 minutes. By using the lid and a lower wattage, the steam stays in to soften the mazonbèl tubers, and Granma does not have to stand over the pot. They are ready when a fork slides through without resistance.

  4. Make the sòs pwa nwa.

    While the mazonbèl finishes, heat olive oil in a separate heavy pot. Soften a few slices of onion, add the épis, and stir until it is fragrant. Add the cooked beans along with their liquid. Nestle the whole scotch bonnet on top. Let it simmer low, lid slightly open, until the sauce thickens.

  5. Serve.

    Drain the mazonbèl tubers. Slice them into rounds, then arrange them on a plate the way Granma likes it — overlapping like a hand of cards. Ladle the sauce on it until she tells you to stop; she usually is generous.

Yield: A meal of unhurried presence.

II. Mazonbèl ak Chiktay Aransò — During the Smoke

For the days when the windows are sealed. The smoke tightens like a tourniquet, cinching homes, lungs, and minds in its now-familiar grip. Each breath wrenched free becomes a rationed triumph. Granma remains indoors.

Ingredients

  • 3 medium Mazonbèl tubers (If the supply from the Caribbean grocery has been uneven, you may already have taro from the Filipino or African markets set aside for days like this.)
  • ½ cup Chiktay Aransò (Spiced Smoked Herring), prepared in advance. A batch has been sitting in a jar in the fridge, the briny flavors mingling into the kind of taste that might wake the appetite. If not for the silent infiltration of smoke.
  • Water, enough to cover by two fingers.

Method

Boil the mazonbèl in a tightly covered pot until fork tender. If using malanga or other taro-like tubers, simmer gently; they soften faster than mazonbèl.

Do not add salt to the boiling water — the chiktay will carry the salt and seasoning.

The aransò topping was made weeks ago by Manman and Granma, back when you all had the time and foresight. Now the house is sealed, the filters overloaded, and reheating anything too fragrant would be overwhelming.

Let the steam do the work. Once the mazonbèl is cooked, spoon the chilled chiktay over the hot pieces and cover the bowl. The rising heat warms everything gently.

Serve.

A portable air purifier runs in the sunroom, a low hum that has become a metronome to the laboured breathing of both Granma and you. Wildfire smoke from fires burning far north has settled over the city. You can taste metal at the back of your throat, shaking your inhaler too many times to count since the skies turned copper.

Yield: A meal of preservation.

III. Té Kanèl — After the Smoke

When the smoke clears and the lungs remember how to expand. Leftovers from the hazy days carry us forward. Fog settled on our brains, not lifted so much as slowly loosening its hold through the slow recovery.

Ingredients

  • 2 sticks of cinnamon (Kanèl) and a piece of star anise
  • A stalk of citronella (lemongrass)
  • Sugar, honey, or syrup — kept on the table, not in the pot. Granma has diabetes; everyone sweetens their own cup.
  • A grieving poem, to be whispered while the water boils:

    mes mains sont racines,
    je tiens les souvenirs
    déterrés pour subsister,
    là où l'air nous terre

Instructions

  1. Boil the cinnamon and anise until the water turns amber.
  2. Serve hot with whatever leftovers carried you through the smoke.
  3. Open the windows. The house laughs, and the rising steam announces the comforting presence of air that no longer needs to be feared. Until the next shift in the air.

Granma eats slowly, her breath still careful, but she is here. We are here. For now, that is enough.

Yield: A cup of renewal.

Prototyping Journal — Why Go From Grid To Graph

This page carries the scaffolding journal: the iteration that produced the Care Grid prototype on Tab 1, the assumptions that were caught and corrected along the way, and what the next iteration is honestly waiting on.

The first prototype — a 16×16 spatial grid

The first version of the Care Grid used a square grid: 256 cells, each with a hidden capacity value (0–100), four discrete states defined by thresholds (regenerating ≥75, buffering 50–75, stressed 25–50, depleted <25). The local rule used a Moore neighbourhood (8 adjacent cells). Each tick, every cell lost some baseline load; cells below buffering then looked at neighbours with surplus and routed support along adjacency. A story panel narrated emerging clusters; buttons applied shocks, amplified baseline load, reset.

Care Grid v1 — spatial grid (preserved for reference)

The original prototype. Click any cell to perturb. States are double-encoded by colour and glyph (● regenerating, ■ buffering, ▲ stressed, ◆ depleted). The quilt-like pattern is genuinely beautiful — but that beauty was hiding a conceptual contradiction.

What the grid did well, and what I keep from it: the four homeodynamic states and their threshold-based sensing; the reciprocal-exchange rule as an ecosocial contract encoded as a local rule; the story panel as warm knowledge rather than dashboard; the absence of a "fix" button (decision lives outside the model); the stress-test affordances (shock, baseline load amplification).

The self-audit — why I moved away from it

The spatial grid was quietly smuggling in geographic adjacency as the topology. That contradicts the whole conceptual move. Care chains span continents (Hochschild's original point); a community hub in one urban context might be a "neighbour" of one in another jurisdiction if they share kinship ties or data-sharing protocols — far more than two physically adjacent neighbourhoods with no meaningful relational contact.

The grid implied that closeness on a map meant something about interaction. The framework says the opposite: adjacency is defined by trust, reciprocity, and shared sensing — not by distance.

I was trained to ask "can you put it on a map?" That question was the threshold of credibility in statistical production. This framework required me to unlearn that default. Geography remains available as an overlay where it aligns with relational ties, but the primary topology is co-defined by the community — who they trust, share with, and sense alongside. I can still make the map. I choose not to let the map be the whole truth.

What changed conceptually in the graph version

  • Adjacency is now relational. Each node has a list of neighbours (edges) that encode actual relationships — kinship, mutual aid, data governance, shared infrastructure.
  • Position emerges from the ties, not the other way around. A force-directed layout (repulsion + attraction weighted by tie strength) places nodes so that strongly connected clusters cluster visually — but the meaningful structure is the edge structure, not the screen coordinates.
  • The exchange rule flows along edges, weighted by tie strength. A weak bridging tie cannot carry as much surplus as a dense, strong tie. That is the mathematical expression of "not all neighbours are equal."
  • Clusters are relational communities, not spatial patches. "Apply shock" hits an entire cluster — a sector-wide funding cut, a regional crisis, a regulatory shift. The story narrates whether the depleted subgraph stays within a cluster (local crisis) or bridges multiple (cascading systemic risk).

Methodological commitments — what is honestly not yet decided

What a fellowship year would commit to

  • The topology is synthetic. Co-design with a community partner would replace the synthetic graph with real nodes, real edges, real tie types and weights. This is the load-bearing commitment.
  • Edge weight is one-dimensional. Real ties are multiplex — a kinship tie is qualitatively different from a data-sharing tie. A more developed version would use a multiplex graph with rules that differ by edge type.
  • The exchange rule is symmetric and undirected. Feminist scholarship on care chains (Hochschild and onward) names that real reciprocity is often directional — obligation flows one way without equivalent return. A more faithful version would use directed edges with asymmetric weights.
  • The clusters are abstract ("Cluster A–D"). A community would name them according to what kind of partition matters in their context.
  • The local rule is mine, not co-designed. The whole point of the methodology is that the rule set is the ecosocial contract. The fellowship would resource the co-design process.
